    <h1>Basil Ajith v. E. Prasad (2021 SCC OnLine SC 836)</h1>
    <p><em>Security Cheques & Section 139 NI Act – What The Supreme Court Said</em></p><h2>Facts of the Case:</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Basil Ajith issued a <strong>post-dated cheque</strong> to E. Prasad as <strong>security</strong> for a loan agreement.</li>
  <li>The cheque was <strong>not issued to repay an existing debt</strong>.</li>
  <li>It got <strong>dishonoured</strong> when presented, and a complaint under <strong>Section 138 of the NI Act</strong> was filed.</li>
  <li>The trial court convicted the appellant.</li>
</ul>


<h2>Legal Issue:</h2>
<p>Can a cheque issued only as <strong>security</strong> attract the <strong>presumption under Section 139</strong> of the NI Act?</p>


<h2>Supreme Court's Verdict:</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Section 139 presumes a legally enforceable debt unless proven otherwise.</li>
  <li>When it is shown that the cheque was issued <strong>only as security</strong>, the presumption does not apply.</li>
  <li>The complainant must prove the existence of an actual <strong>legal liability</strong>.</li>
  <li>The Supreme Court set aside the conviction and <strong>acquitted</strong> Basil Ajith.</li>
</ul>


<h2>Conclusion:</h2>
<p>This case confirms that <strong>security cheques</strong> alone don’t attract Section 138 unless there's proof of <strong>legally enforceable debt</strong>.</p>
